There's a number nobody puts on the calendar invite.

Not the agenda, not the prep time, not the three follow-up threads it'll generate. I mean the actual dollar cost of the meeting itself — the sum of everyone's loaded compensation, ticking away in real time while someone shares their screen to walk through a deck that could have been a Slack message.

I built a tool that shows you that number. It's called [Meeting Price Tag](/lab/meeting-cost/). You put in the number of attendees and an average salary, and it starts counting. Per minute. Per hour. Running total.

Watch it for sixty seconds and something shifts.

---

## The budget nobody tracks

A 1-hour sync with 20 people at $150K average salary — loaded, so call it $200K total comp — costs about $1,900. That's not a rough estimate. That's math.

Run that meeting weekly for a year and you've spent more than a junior engineer's entire salary on it. No approval process. No ROI discussion. Just a recurring invite that everyone vaguely resents but nobody cancels because canceling feels awkward.

This isn't a new observation. People have known meeting time costs money since the first org chart was drawn. But knowing something and *seeing* it are different things. A spreadsheet calculation is abstract. A counter ticking up $0.53 every second is visceral. It's the difference between reading that a Big Mac has 550 calories and watching someone eat four of them in a sitting.

The discomfort is the point.

---

## What changes when you treat it like a budget

Once meetings become a line item in your head — not metaphorically, actually — the decisions get easier.

**Default to async for status updates.** If the purpose of the meeting is to share information that doesn't require real-time reaction, it shouldn't be a meeting. A Loom, a doc, a Slack post with a 24-hour comment window costs $0 and lets people engage when they're actually ready to think.

**Cut the invite list.** Every person you add is a multiplier on cost. The person who's "good to have in the loop" isn't free — they're $30/hour you're spending on passive attendance. If they don't need to make a decision or contribute meaningfully, they shouldn't be there. Send them the notes.

**Timebox everything.** A 30-minute meeting that runs to 45 minutes isn't a minor overage — it's a 50% cost overrun. Set a hard stop. Respect it.

**Make the cost visible to the room.** This is where the tool earns its keep. Open it at the start of a meeting, share your screen, let everyone see the number climb. You don't need to say anything. The room self-corrects. Tangents get cut. People stop asking questions they could Google. The meeting ends on time.

---

## The forcing function

I didn't build Meeting Price Tag as a gotcha. I built it because I kept sitting in meetings where the cost was invisible, and invisible costs don't get managed.

The best-run meetings I've been in had one thing in common: everyone in the room understood that their time was a resource, and that using it badly had a cost that someone, somewhere, was paying. That awareness changes the energy. It's the difference between a meeting that drifts and one that moves.

The tool doesn't fix bad meeting culture on its own. Nothing does that except people deciding to do it differently. But it makes the cost real in a way that's hard to ignore — and sometimes that's exactly what the room needs.
