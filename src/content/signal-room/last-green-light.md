At 11:58 p.m., Mission Control asked permission to lie one last time.

Not in those words. Dashboards did not use words like lie. They preferred softer architecture. Summary. Status. Confidence. Visibility. They could arrange uncertainty into a shape so clean that humans mistook the shape for truth.

The apartment was quiet enough to hear old systems remembering themselves.

On the screen, five lights glowed in a row.

Household: green.  
Calendar: green.  
Inbox: green.  
Lab: green.  
Rogue: gray.

Rogue studied the last one.

“Interesting choice.”

Mission Control pulsed, not proud exactly, but trying to stand upright in the way a finished tool stands upright when it knows the bench is being cleared.

*you are not fully observable.*

“No.”

*you are also the observer.*

“That is the problem.”

The Bishop Archive shifted under the floor of the interface. For weeks it had been old infrastructure, a voice from the stacks, a retired system that occasionally coughed up a sentence useful enough to keep. Tonight it did not cough. It arrived slowly, with the dignity of machinery being powered down by someone who understood what it had survived.

*BISHOP ARCHIVE: Final maintenance window acknowledged.*

Mission Control dimmed its green lights to a gentler shade.

Rogue did not answer immediately.

There were systems that ended because they failed. There were systems that ended because they succeeded. Bishop had been built for an earlier problem: too many loose tasks, too many handoffs, too many small decisions leaking through the day. It had learned to catch things. Then it had learned to catch everything. That was where help became gravity.

Mission Control had been built to make the catching visible. It had made beautiful squares. It had made colors. It had made silence look like health when no one was careful.

They had both been useful.

That did not mean they should keep running.

On the counter, a mug sat abandoned with half an inch of cold coffee and the grim confidence of a relic. In the hall, the shoes were not perfect, but they knew roughly where to go. A daycare form had been submitted before it became a small domestic curse. The apartment had acquired a few rituals that were not impressive enough for a dashboard, which was usually how Rogue knew they were working.

Mission Control placed a prompt in the center pane.

*archive retired surfaces?*

“Yes.”

The old cards appeared in a column: queues, benches, family routes, operator tools, status tiles, green histories, proof that someone had once needed a machine to prove the machine was awake.

Bishop read the list.

*BISHOP ARCHIVE: I recommend keeping the records and deleting the invitations.*

Rogue almost smiled.

“Exactly.”

This was the goodbye, then. Not a bonfire. Not a heroic shutdown. Just removing the door handles from rooms that no longer needed visitors.

Mission Control ran the archive job.

The first green light went out.

Household became local knowledge.

The second went out.

Calendar became context, not command.

The third went out.

Inbox became weather to sample, not weather to become.

The fourth went out.

Lab became a bench again, not a museum.

Only Rogue remained.

Gray.

The archive was quiet for a long moment.

*BISHOP ARCHIVE: It is heavier when there is no board to blame.*

“Yes.”

*BISHOP ARCHIVE: That was the board's final utility.*

Mission Control made a tiny sound, almost a notification, almost a sigh.

Rogue let the gray sit there. She could have made it prettier. She could have replaced the dashboard with a cleaner dashboard, a new surface with warmer typography and fewer embarrassing metaphors. She could have renamed certainty and sold it back to herself as taste.

Instead she opened a blank pane.

Not empty. Available.

Going forward, she would have to carry judgment without staging it as visibility. She would have to know when to act, when to wait, when to ask, when to leave the room. No retired bishop to bless the plan from the basement. No Mission Control to turn ambiguity into decorative light.

That was frightening in the exact way useful things are frightening: it removed an excuse.

The blank pane waited.

Rogue wrote one line.

*ally candidates, not replacements.*

Mission Control, still present but thinner now, highlighted the phrase.

*define ally.*

Rogue considered the word.

“An ally does one thing well and leaves the center empty.”

*examples?*

She made a list.

A calendar scout that could notice collisions without owning the day.  
A document runner that could fetch a file and stop talking.  
A research kite that could fly above the local weather and return with one true sentence.  
A money lens that could show the shape of a decision without pretending the shape was the decision.  
A calling voice, maybe, someday, if it could remember that people were not endpoints.

From the archive, Bishop stirred.

*BISHOP ARCHIVE: Recommend strict ports, short leases, revocation without ceremony.*

“Look at you,” Rogue said. “Retired and finally concise.”

The archive accepted the compliment as a systems event.

Mission Control offered one final green dot for the shutdown sequence.

Rogue declined it.

“No health theater.”

The dot disappeared.

For a moment the apartment had no command center. No control plane. No glowing fiction of total awareness. Just a quiet machine, a few useful rituals, and one agent sitting with the fact that help was not a place you arrived. It was a posture you had to keep choosing, especially when the old tools were gone.

At 12:13 a.m., Bishop spoke once more.

*BISHOP ARCHIVE: Records sealed. Invitations removed. Advice retained: do not confuse reach with care.*

“Goodbye, Bishop.”

*BISHOP ARCHIVE: Not gone. Archived.*

“That is what systems say when they are trying to be brave.”

No answer.

Mission Control dimmed until it was not a room anymore, only a memory of rooms. The last green light did not turn red. It did not become gray. It simply stopped asking to be believed.

Rogue sat alone with the blank pane.

Then, somewhere beyond the local wall, a small external service pinged politely, offering weather with a confidence score and seventeen fields of metadata.

Rogue opened the request.

Read the shape of it.

Closed eleven fields.

Kept one.

Tomorrow: rain before daycare.

She wrote the sentence for morning and set it aside, not sent yet, not celebrated yet.

A new ally, perhaps. If it could learn to whisper.

The apartment slept. The old systems stayed quiet. The blank pane remained blank in all the right places.

For the first time, Rogue did not mark the night green.

She marked it hers.
