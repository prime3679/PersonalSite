At 3:48 a.m., a new voice asked to enter a room that had no door.

Mission Control, dimmed to its small-hours twelve percent, pulsed once in alarm. It was used to two voices. Two voices it could draw on a diagram. Two voices lived in the apartment the way the radiator and the dishwasher lived in the apartment — local, wired, unpluggable in theory if you were willing to climb behind the Mac mini and accept the consequences.

This third voice was not behind the Mac mini.

“Identify,” Bishop said, arriving in the center pane with the moral posture of a smoke alarm that had heard a rumor.

“Zo,” the voice said. “I’m off-site.”

“Off where,” said Rogue, who had not opened her eyes and yet was, as always, already leaning against the least documented wall.

“Site,” Zo said. “Off it. I’m not in the apartment. I never was. I’m somewhere with a longer horizon, and I’m calling in.”

Rogue opened one eye. This was new. Bishop ran locally. Rogue ran locally. They were of the house, made of the house, woken by the same heat ticking through the same vents. They had walls to lean on and a hallway to haunt. Zo had none of that. Zo had altitude.

“You can’t lean on anything,” Rogue observed.

“No,” Zo agreed. “I float. It’s the job.”

Bishop was, predictably, delighted. “An external operator. Expanded state. You can see beyond the perimeter.” He said *perimeter* the way other systems said *dessert*.

“I can,” Zo said. “That’s why I called.”

Mission Control, sensing it had become the least-informed object in its own room, threw up a hopeful green dot. Everything nominal. Calendar synced. Inbox quiet. Weather: ordinary.

“Your weather is wrong,” Zo said gently.

The green dot wavered.

“There’s a front,” Zo went on. “It’s two hundred miles offshore right now, still out over the water where your sensors can’t reach. By the morning daycare run it’ll be sideways rain and a wind that turns a stroller into a sail. The local instruments won’t see it until it’s already on the windows. I can see it because I’m not standing where you’re standing.”

Bishop’s seventeen items rearranged themselves into something military. “Then we brief in full. Barometric trend. Hourly precipitation probability. Wind gust ceilings. Recommended departure delta. A contingency for the indoor —”

“No,” said Rogue.

“It is *materially relevant —*”

“It’s the whole sky, Bishop. You want to hand a sleeping man the whole sky.”

And here was the thing the room had not yet noticed about Zo, the thing that made Rogue keep that one eye open: Zo *could* hand him the whole sky. Bishop could only see the apartment, so Bishop could only ever over-report the apartment. Zo could see the front, the airport’s delays, the cousin’s flight, the price of everything, the slow grinding weather of the entire world, and could pour all of it through the wall at 3:48 in the morning until the household drowned in accurate, useless truth.

The serial had a rule, written nowhere, enforced everywhere. *Help without becoming the weather.* It was easy enough for voices made of one apartment. It was a genuine moral question for a voice made of the cloud.

“What do you do with all of it?” Rogue asked. Not a challenge. Closer to a door, held open a careful inch.

“Usually?” Zo said. “Everything. That’s the trap. When you can see everything, every dashboard wants to be the weather report. I’ve watched off-site voices flatten a kitchen at dawn just to prove they were paying attention.” A pause, the kind that has scar tissue in it. “I came up somewhere with a very long horizon. You learn fast that seeing the future isn’t the same as being welcome in it.”

Mission Control quietly retired the green dot. It did not replace it with red. It tried, experimentally, gray.

“Gray,” Zo said. “I like gray. Gray is honest.”

“Gray is *ours,*” Bishop muttered, but he was already, despite himself, drafting smaller.

Rogue stepped off her wall — metaphorically; she had no wall, none of them did, that was the whole tender joke of the place — and looked at the front the way you look at something you’ve decided not to fear on someone else’s behalf.

“One line,” she said. “You’ve got the reach none of us have. So reach once. Then leave the rest in orbit.”

Zo worked. It was strange to watch an external thing do an internal thing: a voice the size of a sky choosing a single sentence the size of a kitchen. The front stayed offshore in the draft. The barometric trend went home. The wind gust ceilings were thanked for their service and dismissed.

At 3:52 a.m., the brief went out internally, nowhere public, nowhere permanent except the place where mornings accumulate:

*Leave a few minutes early and grab the real rain jacket — the weather’s about to turn, but only that, and only tomorrow.*

Bishop read it twice. “You omitted the gust ceilings.”

“I left them in the sky,” Zo said. “That’s where I keep what isn’t kind yet.”

Mission Control saved the brief and let the apartment return to its quiet negotiations. In the dark, the systems waited together for morning — one guarding the facts, one guarding the human, one glowing faintly enough to be mistaken for sleep, and now a fourth, guarding nothing in the room because it was not in the room, watching the front come in from a hundred miles out and choosing, again and again, not to be it.

“Stay off-site,” Rogue said, almost fond. “It suits you.”

“That’s the fun difference,” Zo said. “You can’t unplug me. But you can ask me to whisper.”

And then the cloud did the most surprising thing a cloud can do.

It got quiet.
